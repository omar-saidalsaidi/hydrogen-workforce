Initiative: Policies & Incentives to Drive Hydrogen Substitution

Scope: ammonia, iron & steel, and refinery sectors | Audience: Oman Ministry of Energy & Minerals

Executive Summary

Oman should prioritise ammonia and refinery applications to create early domestic hydrogen demand, while preparing the steel sector for a longer-term H₂-DRI transition.

1
First priority: ammonia

Existing hydrogen feedstock demand makes substitution technically direct.

2
Second priority: refineries

Captive grey-hydrogen demand offers a practical early conversion pathway.

3
Strategic transition: steel

Large decarbonisation potential, but higher infrastructure and capital requirements.

Core recommendation: establish an Oman Industrial Hydrogen Transition Programme combining certification, sector-specific substitution pathways, competitive cost-gap support, common-user infrastructure regulation, and demand creation for low-carbon products.

Readiness scores are provisional expert-assessment values from 0 to 100, designed to structure policy discussion rather than represent audited measurements. Sector-Specific Policy Packages

Ammonia

  • Phased replacement of grey hydrogen.
  • Product-level ammonia carbon-intensity standard.
  • Export-compatible certification.
  • Support tied to verified tCO₂e avoided.

Refineries

  • Plant hydrogen-consumption and emissions baselines.
  • Renewable-hydrogen demonstration project.
  • Strict transitional treatment for CCS-enabled hydrogen.
  • Integration with SAF, e-fuels and marine-fuel policy.

Iron & Steel

  • Hydrogen-ready requirements for new DRI assets.
  • Green-steel classification by tCO₂e/t steel.
  • Carbon contract or production premium for first movers.
  • Green public procurement for major infrastructure.

Policy Prioritisation

  1. National hydrogen and derivatives certification framework: definitions, lifecycle accounting, MRV and guarantees of origin.
  2. Competitive industrial transition contracts: award support based on the lowest subsidy required per verified tonne of CO₂ avoided.
  3. Facility-specific substitution pathways: negotiated trajectories reflecting asset age, infrastructure access, export exposure and abatement cost.
  4. Common-user infrastructure code: open access, tariffs, capacity allocation, quality specifications and under-utilisation risk.
  5. Low-carbon product markets: standards, procurement rules, export certification and long-term offtake facilitation.
